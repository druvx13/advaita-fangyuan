Advaita Vedānta and Fang Yuan

Desire, Detachment, and Immortality

This exploration contrasts two profound philosophical approaches to desire, detachment, and the pursuit of immortality: the ancient wisdom of Advaita Vedānta from Hindu tradition and the modern fictional character Fang Yuan from the cultivation novel Reverend Insanity.

Advaita Vedānta

The Nature of Bondage

In Advaita Vedānta, the bondage of saṃsāra is rooted in kāmāḥ – the desires and attachments that arise from ignorance (avidyā) of one's true Self.

"yadā sarve pramucyante kāmā ye 'sya hṛdi śritāḥ ... atha mṛtyo 'mṛto bhavaty atra Brahma samaśnute"

"When all the desires that dwell in the heart fall away, then the mortal becomes immortal and here attains Brahman."

— Katha Upaniṣad 2.3.14

This verse encapsulates the Advaita teaching that only by renouncing kāma can the jīva (individual soul) realize its identity with Brahman, the universal Self.

Desire as the Enemy

In Bhagavad Gītā, Krishna warns that "kāmaḥ eṣa krodha eṣa" – lust is born of contact with passion, and when thwarted becomes wrath... Know this to be the all-devouring enemy (vairiṇa) of the wise.

In Vedantic metaphysics, kāma (often including krodha – anger and lobha – greed) fuels the cycle of births.

The Path to Liberation

As Katha Upaniṣad 2.1.2 explains, "as long as [he] cherishes desires ... he goes through the round of births and deaths. But when he realizes himself to be infinite Ātman ... he then becomes free from ignorance, desire, and action, and is emancipated from death".

Thus Vedānta holds that ahamkāra (ego) and its vasanas (mental residues) are superimpositions upon the Self; by jñāna (self-knowledge) one dispels these illusions.

The Nature of Immortality

The śāstras declare ātman as sat-cit-ānanda (existence-consciousness-bliss) and identical to Brahman (mahāvākya aham brahmāsmi).

In this state of realization the false dichotomy of "mortal–immortal" vanishes: the liberated being experiences amata (deathlessness) here and now, even while living (jīvanmukta).

Key Teachings

Desire and Bondage

Desire (kāma) is repeatedly called "all-devouring" and the root of bondage. Advaita sees all action born of desire as tainted by rajas and tamas (ignorance).

Nonduality and Ego-Death

Advaita asserts an absolute non-duality (advaita): the apparent multiplicity is mithyā (neither true nor false reality). The "dropping of egoic identity" is the pivot.

Mokṣa as Immortality

Mokṣa in Vedānta literally means release or immortality (amṛta). The "mortal" refers to the limited ego-self; the "immortal" is the Self (ātman) understood as Brahman.

The Path

Shankara comments that only in calmness of mind (śānta) does the light of pure consciousness (cit) shine unobstructed. One must cultivate vairāgya (dispassion) to realize Brahman.

Fang Yuan's Path in Reverend Insanity

The Cultivation World

Reverend Insanity (蜀山传奇 Gui Dao) spins a complex Daoist-flavored cultivation world where mastery of Gu (magical insects) confers powers up to Gu Xianzun (Gu Immortal) and Demon Venerable levels.

Key concepts include karma, reincarnation, and celestial balance (Heaven's Will), albeit remixed in a nihilistic narrative. Souls experience karma ("Heaven's Will" 天意) not as moral judgment but as cosmic equilibrium (天道).

Fang Yuan's Goal

Fang Yuan, the "Mad Demon," is a transmigrator who has lived hundreds of years. His explicit goal is eternal life – a higher plane beyond ordinary Gu immortals.

"Only eternal life, this majestic and unattainable target, could make the journey of his life become more interesting. ... He calmly accepted death and failure. ... In the process of pursuing eternal life, he found his meaning."

All base emotions – "the lowly lust and desires of his body, satisfaction of love and hatred" – have lost their appeal, and he now considers "only eternal life ... worthy".

Moral Detachment

Unlike a sectarian cultivator, Fang Yuan feels no filial piety or friendship binding him. He has abandoned Confucian/moral constraints; in one confrontation he sneers:

"What is a sin? ... Committing a sin means one deserves death – only the weaklings' naive ideology."

He gladly identifies as a "demonic head" who will "slaughter living beings and enjoy all that I can get". His calculus is pure self-interest and utilitarianism: any action is justified if it advances his goals.

Detachment and Cynicism

On the surface, Fang Yuan behaves with cold detachment: he considers emotions "boring". Yet Fang's detachment is not spiritual; it is a pragmatic armor.

He often speaks of defying cosmic order: "Defy heaven, defy fate, defy the universe!" His maxim is survival by the fittest: "I am here for my own interests... If an immortal blocks me, I will slay the immortal."

Key Aspects of Fang Yuan's Philosophy

Cultivation Logic

The world is essentially Darwinian. Cultivators seek immortality through personal effort (every opportunistic cheat is fair). Reincarnation is concrete: Fang resets his life using the Spring Autumn Cicada Gu.

Metaphysics

Fang Yuan's metaphysics is the flip side of destiny: he denies any moral meaning in suffering and uses karma as a resource rather than a restraint. He destroys "calamity Gu" to escape Heaven's interference.

Pursuit of Immortality

His pursuit of "eternal detachment" is not Advaitic renunciation but a will-to-power and rebellion: he embraces only his desire for immortality and the freedom to pursue it beyond all bounds.

Self-Reliance

"The answer to resolving problems is in our own hands," he declares, resonating with a crude self-reliance. He speaks of a "miraculous heavenly power" residing within one's own heart.

Comparative Analysis

Parallel Detachment?

At first glance Fang Yuan's disregard for love and hatred mirrors the Vedāntic ideal of virāga. Like the sage who "does not covet any uncertain thing", Fang has renounced worldly attachments. His emotional state is eerily calm; he has "no ripples" on his soul.

However, key differences emerge: Advaita's dispassion is rooted in realization of the Self, whereas Fang's is tactical boredom. He is "tired" of lower desires, yet this exhaustion simply refocuses him on a new desire – absolute immortality.

In Advaita, once ātman is known, desires naturally dissolve; but Fang still desires (चाह), albeit a loftier object. His desire is no longer for sensual pleasure, but for the impossible (amatum). Vedanta would say this is merely subtle kāma, anvasana of ego: he still identifies as Fang Yuan, still clings to "I".

Morality and Karma

Vedantic philosophy emphasizes nitya-dharma (eternal righteousness) and compassion as outgrowths of Self-knowledge. Self-realized sages naturally love all beings as themselves.

Fang Yuan, by contrast, outright rejects any moral law. Where Arjuna is called to transcend selfish desire (BG 3.43), Fang declares "love and friendship ... boring". He enjoys suffering inflicted on others ("fabulous that they died").

He mocks the notion of "sin" as only a tool of the weak. His ethos is adamantine egotism: he expects neither divine nor karmic retribution on moral grounds, only strategic setbacks.

Freedom vs. Surrender

The Upanishadic ideal of mokṣa is freedom through surrender to Brahman, ultimately seeing no agent but the Self. Fang Yuan's "freedom" is through conquering – dominion over Heaven's Will and the world.

He "defies fate" rather than dissolving in it. From a Vedantic viewpoint, Fang has not attained the requisite avidya-nivritti (removal of ignorance); instead, he intensifies his ego-will.

His statements attempt a Spinozistic turn, but always with Fang at the center. He seems to echo Krishnamurti or Daoist self-cultivation platitudes, but does not actually transcend self-concern.

Do All Desires Fall Away?

The crux: Upaniṣadic renunciation demands sarva-kāmāḥ (all desires) fall away. Fang does cast off ordinary kama and even krodha, but his will to suṣūpṇa-āyuḥ (everlasting life) remains.

One could argue that in his mind the desire for mortality is merely replaced by desire for immortality – hardly pramucyante sarve kāmāḥ. He even admits life's meaning came from the process of pursuing immortality.

Thus he has not truly let go of desire; he has simply "upgraded" it. In Upanishadic terms, Fang is not a jīvanmukta: he still plays the roles of this world (as hero, demon, rebel) and has not realized the sat (truth) that "all is one".

Immortality: Brahman or Simulacrum?

Advaita promises amṛtatva (deathlessness) through union with Brahman. This is not ordinary life extension but a metaphysical transformation of identity. In contrast, Fang Yuan literally seeks physical or Gu-given immortality.

He attains the 11th-rank Demon Venerable stage. Yet this immortality remains in duality: Fang remains "Fang Yuan" with desires (though few). His soul carouses across lives, never at peace.

Vedanta distinguishes amṛta as the essence of ātman (inner void bliss) from amṛtatva as mere longevity. Fang's immortality is more akin to what the Heavens term "修炼到天人合一" – a power-state — but he has not attained nirguṇa-paramārtha.

In the Upaniṣads, true immortality is in ānanda (bliss) and equanimity, not in endless action. Fang's every step is still within saṃsāra (rebirth). Thus we must ask: is Fang's "immortality" an alignment with Brahman or a simulacrum?

Summary of Contrasts

Aspect Advaita Vedānta Fang Yuan
Goal Union with Brahman Personal immortality and power
Desire Elimination of all kāma leads to Brahman Elimination of sensual desires but obsessive kāma for immortality persists
Self vs No-Self Dissolution of ego (ahamkāra) into nondual Self Ego remains, even strengthens ("my interests", "I will...")
Morality Transcendence of good/evil through wisdom and compassion Rejection of all morality as hindrance
Immortality Realized inner immortality, beyond life and death Literal endless life cycle, still mortal in essence

Conclusion

Fang Yuan and Reverend Insanity serve as a dark counterpoint to Advaita Vedānta. Fang's arc suggests that mere detachment or cynicism without surrendering the ego is not true liberation.

If all desires truly fall away (as the Upaniṣad claims), one attains Brahman – an undying witness far beyond worldly schemes. Fang attains something else: a synthetic immortality of his own devising, admired by none but an eternal game-player.

The Upanishadic wisdom thus remains both illuminated and interrogated by his tale: it is not enough to denounce kāma in principle – one must annihilate the substratum of separateness from which all kāma springs.

Classical scriptures and commentaries (Katha Upaniṣad, Bhagavad Gītā) and select passages from Reverend Insanity (Fang Yuan's thoughts and dialogues). Each provides insight into desire, the self, and what "immortality" truly means.
